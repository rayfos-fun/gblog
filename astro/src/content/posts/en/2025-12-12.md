Recently, I received that dreaded piece of mail: a summons for Jury Duty.

I had a scheduling conflict and postponed it. Later, technology failed me. The court's website was glitching, and I missed the mandatory online check-in window. Not wanting to be a fugitive from justice (okay, maybe that's dramatic), I went to the courthouse in person the next day to explain myself.

### The Verdict: "You're Free to Go"

I walked up to the clerk, ready to stumble through an explanation about server errors and login failures. We exchanged maybe two sentences. The clerk paused, looked at me with a knowing glance, and started typing rapidly.

Just like that, my summons was canceled. Dismissed.

The official reason? **Language Barrier.**

It was a bittersweet victory. On one hand, congratulations to me! I dodged a week of sitting in a courtroom. On the other hand... ouch. It’s a bit of an ego bruise to be officially labeled as "not fluent enough."

### The "Bay Area Bubble" Reality

Deep down, I know the clerk was right. My English is functional—I can handle my job, discuss code, and get by in daily life. But throw me into a room full of legal jargon, cultural slang, and rapid-fire debates? I'd be completely lost.

I originally thought I’d give it a shot just for the experience—something to brag about later. But it seems my destiny as a juror ends here.

Reflecting on this, I realized something: I've been in the U.S. for 10 years. If my English was going to reach native-level fluency, it would have happened by now.

Does it make life hard? Honestly, not really. Especially here in the Bay Area. We live in a bubble where I can walk into my favorite restaurants and order in Mandarin.

### Making Peace with Imperfection

When I first arrived in the States, I stressed about "perfecting" my English. I wanted to master every idiom and lose the accent. But over time, I realized that **good enough is actually good enough.**

As long as I can communicate at work (even if it involves some wild hand gestures) and connect with people, that’s what matters. I’d rather spend my limited time on things I’m truly passionate about than obsessing over grammar textbooks.

Besides, with AI translation getting better every day, the language gap is shrinking.

Getting rejected from jury duty was a reminder: I don't need to force myself to fit every mold. Accepting my limitations allows me to live more comfortably in my own skin.

So, maybe my English isn't courtroom-ready. But hey, at least it got me a free week back. I’ll take that as a win.
